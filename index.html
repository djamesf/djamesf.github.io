The shared understanding of program concepts involves the collaborative construction of the technical knowledge needed to build programs. A successful collaborative strategy for collaboratively building knowledge is the JIGSAW technique (Kordaki and Siempos 2011). Jigsaw is a productive learning strategy where students explain the subject they studied to someone else, usually their peers. The Jigsaw technique divides classes into mixed groups to work on small problems that the group assembles into a final result. For example, a classroom assignment is divided into topics. Students are then divided into groups with one member assigned to each topic.

Working individually, each student learns about their topic and presents it to their group. Then, students gather in groups divided by topic. Each member presents again to the topic group. In groups on the same topic, students reconcile points of view and synthesize information. They create a final report. Finally, the original groups come together and listen to each member's performance. Final presentations provide all group members with an understanding of their own material, as well as the findings that emerged from group discussion of specific topics.

As an example of a programming concept that must be built collectively, using the JIGSAW technique, we have the concept of program correction. Kolikant (2005) examined students' definition of correction, especially as it relates to tests. She found that students believe that a proper testing strategy includes testing every example they can think of, which is a strategy that instructors find incomplete.

The definition of correction used in Kolikant's research (2005) shows that the program should not present: syntax errors, logical errors and runtime errors (Jackson, 1996). Syntax errors will occur when a programmer misuses the syntax of a programming language. Logical errors (or semantic errors) will occur when a programmer's logical thinking is not correct enough. Runtime errors will occur in many situations. For example, if a programmer does not notice the domain of a variable's value, a runtime error ''overflow/underflow' may be encountered. In this phase, students must plan collaborative activities, setting up a task flow to be performed. Students should also discuss to reach consensus on the division of labor and time management (Zheng & Yu, 2016).

Scrum is a suitable method for managing collaborative programming tasks. Scrum is an agile method, based on an English sport called “Rugby” where players on a team are stacked, forming a wall. The method presupposes that, for a project to succeed, the people involved must always work together, engaged in a common objective. The method establishes three types of actors in the process, with their respective profiles.

● Scrum Master: Is the project leader, which facilitates communication, enabling the necessary conditions for the execution of tasks. The leader, who directs and facilitates the actions necessary for the success of the project.

● Product Owner: Is who represents the project's stakeholders.

● Team: Represented by the development team, that is, the people who develop the project. It is the actions of this team that determine the success of the project. With the participants well defined, the sprints are defined, established deadlines for the teams (students) to present their tasks, presenting the partial results of the project in question. A sprint is a short, limited period in which a Scrum team works to complete a certain amount of work. Sprints are at the heart of the Scrum method, and getting the sprints right will help your agile team deliver better software with less setbacks.

Tasks must be well defined and clear and must be organized into a list of priorities, the backlog. In agile development, a product backlog is a prioritized list of deliverables (such as new features) that must be implemented as part of a project or product development. It's a decision-making artifact that helps estimate, refine, and prioritize everything the team might want to accomplish in the future.

Summarizing the Scrum method in steps:

● Choose your roles: The team needs a Product Owner (has final say on project needs), a Scrum Master (helps the team take action and communicate), and team members. Remember, there is no room for egos in Scrum. Scrum works on a "leader serving the group" model.

● Create the Product Backlog: The Backlog is where the team will list everything the project needs, ordered by importance. Keep in mind that the Backlog is never complete. As the project takes shape, new needs arise and you add to the Backlog. The Product Owner is primarily responsible for this.

● Plan your Sprints: Next, it's time to choose the Backlog tasks to complete in your first Sprint. Sprints have a time limit, which the team mutually defines. During sprint planning, the team decides which tasks to include in the sprint, how long it will take to complete them, and who will be responsible for them. Create a visual board: Create a visual board to track actions by updating the board with each completed sprint.

● Get to work: It's time to get to work on this Sprint! Team members work on their tasks and everyone checks in on their progress.

● Reviewing Their Work: At the end of the Sprint, the team reviews the work done and presents its completed tasks. Each Sprint ends with a Review, or Retrospective, where the team reviews its work and discusses ways to improve the next Sprint.

● Repeat: With the first Sprint over, it's time to start all over again. Choose more Backlog tasks and repeat the process.

One of the fundamental characteristics of the Scrum methodology, and what makes it so potentially powerful, is the idea of repetition (or iteration) and improvement. This works both in relation to the product to be worked on and the efficiency of the team itself. At the end of each Sprint, the work should be ready to be handed over to a customer (in an educational context, the customer might be the teacher). This does not mean that it is a finished and complete project. Far from it. Rather, it means that the work must be sufficient to show some sort of completed “Minimally Viable Product” (MVP, in startup parlance).

Scrum is a good possibility to actively include students in the teaching-learning process. Appoint them as executors of a project, either for a project with a specific objective or even transforming the subject itself into a project with several actions to be carried out (works, tasks, assessments, etc.) engaging learning experiences. The Scrum method can help students assess the amount of work done, compliance with the release of activities in plans, productivity and ability to estimate effort (Mahnic, 2012). Effort regulation has a significant correlation with student performance in the learning process.

Empirical data showed that the Scrum method has the potential to be successful in educational projects due to its characteristics of adaptive planning, evolutionary development and flexible responses to changes. Estimating and planning are difficult tasks, but they can get better with practice.

Socio-cognitive Dimension

This dimension addresses the awareness and monitoring of collective activities for possible adjustments. Awareness is a precondition for monitoring the group progress, generally subsumed as students’ perception and knowledge about a certain situation or as the understanding of the activities of others, which provides a context for our own activity (Giglers et al., 2013).

Monitoring group progress involves the detection of wrong ideas, paths, and cognitive conflicts, as well as validation behaviors as a reaction to the work progress and shared understanding. On one hand, cognitive conflict occurs when a student reject, deny or give a negative answer to/evaluation of what been sad or done before. On the other hand, acceptance takes place when learners agree to what been said or done without further elaboration or when they adopt the perspectives of their peers and build syntheses of various arguments and counterarguments that learning partners have presented before (Omid et al., 2013).

To increase learners’ awareness of their own and others’ learning process, Järvelä et al. Coding Dojo is a powerful strategy for socially shared regulation in programming. A Coding Dojo is a meeting where a group of programmers gather to learn, practice and share experiences. The Dojo plays an important role in learning to code, creating an environment to foster and share agile practices like Test Driven Development, Refactoring and Pair Programming. The main objective of a Coding Dojo is to learn through practice (Sato et al., 2008).

A Coding Dojo is a periodic (usually weekly) meeting organized around a coding challenge where people are encouraged to participate and share their coding skills with the public as they solve the problem. The main principles of the Coding Dojo are to create a safe environment that is collaborative, inclusive and non-competitive, where people can learn continuously. Some of the XP principles align well with this, such as Failure – it’s okay to fail when learning something new – Redundancy – you can always gain new insights by approaching the same problem with different strategies – and Baby Steps – each step towards success. solution must be small enough that everyone can understand it and replicate it later.

Sessions generally follow the same process:

• Choice of Issues (5-10 minutes): Prior to the meeting, participants receive an email with 3-5 choices of issues to be resolved. Problems are chosen from various sources (such as Ruby Quiz and Coding Challenges). Each option is presented briefly and participants vote on which problem will be solved.

• Problem Discussion (10-20 minutes): Once the problem is chosen, the group discusses the different approaches to solving it, usually ending with an agreed approach and a to-do list, to guide the peers through implementation.

• Coding Session (1-2 hours): With an agreed approach to solving the problem, participants begin the coding session in one of two formats – Prepared Kata or Randori. They should practice pair programming and test-driven development as a general rule.

• Retrospective (10-20 minutes): At the end of the session, participants stop coding (even if the problem has not been completely resolved) to reflect on the experience and share lessons learned with the group.

Finally, the Coding Dojo introduced two special roles that can be switched between participants, but which are very important for organizing and ensuring that meetings keep happening. The Moderator or Organizer is responsible for what happens before, during and after the meeting. It handles tasks like reserving the meeting room, sending reminders and options for issues to be resolved, setting up the computer and projector before the meeting, moderating discussions, conducting the retrospective, and cleaning up the room after the session. The Scribe is responsible for publishing the results of the session and sharing them with people who were unable to attend the meeting. He handles tasks like posting the session report, posting the final source code to the group, sometimes taking photos and documenting the results of the retrospective.

Participants are encouraged to develop the solution using Test Driven Development and are free to choose the programming language they prefer. There are two main meeting formats:

• Prepared Kata: In this format, someone has already solved the proposed Kata before the meeting (alone or in a group) and the solution is presented to the audience during the session. Instead of showing the final code and tests, the presenters start from scratch, explaining each step and allowing other participants to ask questions or suggestions. The goal of the session is for everyone to be able to reproduce the steps and solve the same problem after the meeting.

• Randori: In this format, participants solve the problem together, following TDD and Pair Programming in time-boxed rounds (usually between 5 and 7 minutes). At the end of each shift, the pilot joins the audience, the co-pilot becomes the pilot and a new co-pilot joins the audience duo. An extra rule is that discussions and suggestions should only be given when the pair reaches a green bar, with all current tests passing. The reason is that while at a red bar, the pair must focus and work together to pass the tests. The public can always suggest refactorings and optimizations in a green bar.

Socio-contextual Dimension

In more student-centered classrooms, such as communities of learners, classrooms and project-based instruction, students are asked to do much more actual control and regulation of the academic tasks and classroom climate and structure (Agricola et al, 2020). They often are asked to design their own projects and experiments, design how their groups will collect data or perform the task, develop classroom norms for discourse and thinking, and even work together with the teacher to determine how they will be evaluated on the tasks. Of course, this does not mean that developmentally all students, especially those in the early elementary years, are able to regulate the academic tasks, classroom context, and themselves, but these types of classrooms do highlight the potential types of contextual regulation that is possible in the classroom context.

A productive communication strategy that can be used to discuss the use of control structures in programming is the collaborative learning technique of discourse structuring called academic controversy (Johnson, Johnson, & Smith, 1996). For example, to decide which Selection framework, IF or CASE, is more appropriate for a teacher-given problem, academic controversy can be used.

Controversies arise when people's opinions differ on matters considered important by all involved. Perhaps the most cited benefit of controversy is that it stimulates thought. Dewey and Bentley (1949) explain: “Conflict is the fly of thought. It stirs us with observation and memory. Stimulates invention. He shocks us with the passivity of a sheep and makes us notice and invent... Conflict is a sine qua non of reflection and ingenuity”. Controversy can also provide a glimpse into the complexity of the real world and encourage tolerance for ambiguity. Encounters with a variety of viewpoints on a complex issue can lead students to re-examine and possibly revise their own ideas.

The basic steps in academic controversy are:

1. Students are in groups of four divided into pairs. Each pair is given one of two positions on a controversial topic, such as how humans should eat non-human animals. For example, one group of two might be assigned the position that we should eat our animal companions, while the other two should argue for the view that we shouldn't eat them. Students have time to prepare to present the best possible case for their assigned position, regardless of what their personal opinion is. By sharing and developing ideas with their peers, students can realize the value of cooperating with others.

2. Each pair takes turns presenting their assigned vision while the other two listen and take notes.

3. Each side engages in refuting the points made by the other side and defending the position they have advanced. Here is one of the places in the technique that can generate cognitive conflict. In addition, students need to think deeply to defend their assigned view, with facts, examples, and reasons.

4. Here is where academic controversy begins to differ from the typical competitive debate. Students switch positions and repeat Steps 1, 2, and 3 with the new assigned position. Again, they try their best to represent their assigned position. Just to be clear, Step 4 is actually three steps, as students repeat Steps 1, 2, and 3, but this time they represent the view they had previously argued against.

5. Students are no longer part of a group of two, nor are they assigned a role. Instead, in their group of four, they try to forge a common position, which could be one of the two views presented earlier and could also be another position on the issue. For example, they may decide that eating meat is fine, but it should only be done once a week. Students prepare to share this vision with others. If a group fails to reach consensus despite a sincere effort to do so, they are prepared to report the various opinions of group members.

The academic controversy in programming is exemplified below.

Individually: Students are in groups of four divided into pairs. Each pair is assigned one of two positions on a controversial topic, in this case using IF or CASE. Students are given time to prepare to present the best possible case for the assigned position, regardless of what their personal opinion is. One student from the first pair must look for arguments favorable to the use of the IF structure and the other student from the first pair must look for arguments against the use of the CASE structure. While, a student of the first pair must look for arguments favorable to the use of the CASE structure and the other student of the first pair must look for arguments against the use of the IF structure. The arguments must be based on theory and practice, and at least two versions of the algorithms must be implemented, one for each control structure.

In Group:

Each pair presents their point of view, while the other listens carefully and takes notes.

Each pair tries to refute the other's argument.

The group is reintegrated into four students, who evaluate the pros and cons and try to reach a consensus. If the group does not reach a consensus, they must prepare a report containing several points of view.

Individually reflect on:

With the use of the academic controversy technique, was it possible to examine and revise my ideas?

Did the use of the academic controversy technique allow me to envision and compare different solutions to the same problem, helping me to deal with ambiguity in different solutions?

Was it stimulating to research, apprehend, present and refute different points of view?

Do I intend to suggest the academic controversy technique in future group work?

Phase 4. Reaction and Reflection - Reflections on program coding

Socio-cognitive Dimension

The behavioral processes covered in this dimension encompass the triggering, bringing together, evaluating, criticizing ideas. Here, it is evoked Dewey and Bentley’s (1949) notion of transactional inquiry to elaborate the creativity concept in a dialogic way. When participants engage in inquiry together, new meanings are created as a co-production. Also, by means of evaluations of ideas students are able to carry out decision making processes based on criteria application and improve ideas considering its bad features.

Newman et al. (2004) corroborates this assumption. They advocate that the evaluation of alternative solutions and new ideas within a social context is essential for the development of good solutions. This needs judgmental skills of making decisions, statements, appreciations, evaluations and criticisms or "sizing up".

Guilford (1950) reinforces the importance of reflection for creativity. He highlights the importance of the ability to consider and reconsider, to evaluate our ideas as well as the ideas of others; to take time to achieve understanding and insight, to look ahead and plan, and to visualize the complete picture.

One important aspect of this phase and dimension is that when students evaluate and critique different perspectives and ideas they must be confronted with uncertainty and conceptual conflict. Both are states of disequilibrium that activate a process of conflict resolution and a quest for certainty (Dewey & Bentley, 1949) Here, the students are supposed to come up with, deepens and widens new ideas. They jointly build new knowledge by evaluating, comparing, selecting concepts and ideas, considering different alternatives, pointing positive and negative outcomes based in criteria application, starting a search for a more adequate cognitive perspective and reasoning process aiming to resolve conflict and uncertainty.

Järvelä et al. (2016), propose a reflection on group discussions based on the reasons for the results obtained by the group. Group members should examine the explanations from the given options for decision making about the knowledge building.

In programming, joint reflection can explore different student solutions. According to Barr and Stephenson (2011), in relation to computational thinking, students need to understand that problems can be solved in several ways, having “flexibility tolerance” is a skill to be acquired. To show students that a computational problem can have multiple solutions, a collaborative strategy to share multiple solutions is alternative work (Chorfi et al., 2020), where different learners can develop alternative versions of the same code fragment. Such versions can be discussed below.

Examples of questions to think about comparing different versions of programs:

• Did evaluating different solutions together help you understand that there can be more than one program to solve the same computational problem?

• Did sharing different points of view help us in learning to program?

• Are we more confident that our solutions are correct?

• Did sharing different points of view help everyone in the group improve the quality of the code?

• Sharing knowledge allowed us to share skills with our partners?

• Was there faster correction of logic and syntax errors?

• Do we feel responsible about participating in tasks?

Socio-emotional Dimention

For the co-regulation of motivation and affect, the assessment of the emotional aspects of the group members, with respect to mutual respect and engagement in group activities is an important step (Webster, 2020). Previous performance measures past performances with the current performance. Normative criteria, contrary to mastery or previous performance, makes comparisons within a social group, i.e., about other’s performances. Finally, the criteria for collaborative evaluations involve making a team evaluation, which changes depending on different team endeavors. Järvelä et al. (2016), propose rating the motivations, searching for explanations, and evaluating their consequences for learning aiming further planning.

Some questions that can be used by students for joint reflection are:

• Do we need to better respect the ideas of our colleagues?

• Do we need to commit more to collaborative program development? • How are you interacting in the group?

• How can we improve group interactivity?

• How is the dynamic of group participation?

• What can we do to improve everyone's participation? Students should reflect as a group on how their studies:

• If everyone could solve their doubts. Why?

• If everyone is doing the work. Why?

• If there are any patterns of behavior currently in the group that are bothering anyone.

• Whether connectivity within the group should be managed, ie try to see if people are happy with the group's behavior and try to resolve the issue if any. • It should be checked whether group members feel good about other members. Why?

• If you think that the group's evolution is good, be it in interpersonal relationships as well as those related to the work itself. Why?

It is important to try to help colleagues solve their problems empathetically. Try to understand what the group's objectives are and, with them in hand, try to integrate them so that everyone benefits. Manage multiple conflicting goals and objectives, trying to reach agreement on which goal to accomplish first. For example, the following phrases should be used to promote reflection:

• Are you enjoying interacting with the group? Why?

•Is the way we interact in the group cool? Why?

• Are you enjoying the tasks performed? If not, what kind of tasks would you like to do?

•Are the tasks too easy or difficult? How can we help you?

•Are the tasks meeting your objectives? If not, we can help.

• Are you meeting your goals? If not, we can help.

Socio-behavioral Dimention

This dimension involves the evaluation of the group with respect to the amount of interactions and with how many different people interacted. Reflection on student participation is important for collective decisions about changing group strategies (Kaplan et al. 2017).

As previously stated, the Scrum method can be used to manage collaborative programming tasks and define roles to be performed by members, facilitating group interactions.

Some processes and questions that facilitate reflection during and after using Scrum are:

• At the end of the Sprint, the team reviews the work done and presents its completed tasks. Each Sprint ends with a Review, or Retrospective, where the team reviews its work and discusses ways to improve the next Sprint.

• Did Scrum help deliver small portions of the larger product and provide a flexible response to any changes deemed important by the team?

• Has the team's planning ability improved from Sprint to Sprint?

• Has Scrum helped reduce work waste, increase productivity and increase efficiency?

• Was the team able to dynamically adjust the level of work in progress to avoid being idle?

• Were the visual boards useful for coordination between the team and was the progress of tasks monitored?

• Has making work visible, especially seeing work-in-progress bottlenecks, lead to increased communication and collaboration?

• Did making the work visible help the team build improved team knowledge as a form of shared mental model?

Socio-Contextual Dimention

During collaborative learning, the students often are asked to design their own projects and experiments, work together in groups, design how their groups will collect data or perform the task, develop classroom norms for discourse and thinking, and even work together with the teacher to determine how they will be evaluated on the tasks. These types of classrooms obviously offer a great deal more autonomy and responsibility to the students and they provide multiple opportunities for reflections aiming contextual control and regulation, such as adapting group roles and communication protocols (Webster, 2020). Järvelä et al. (2016), propose rating the behaviors, searching for explanations, and evaluating their consequences for learning aiming further planning.

Collaborative writing of program code is a strategy for self-regulation, co-regulation and socially shared regulation of context. Role assignment in collaborative writing provides an opportunity for participants to write, query, annotate, and comment on specific parts of shared code, allowing multiple cognitive strategies to be applied simultaneously. Roles can be fixed or dynamically changed. Four types of roles can be played by students when writing a program: writer, consultant, editor, and reviewer, as discussed earlier. Roles can be reallocated if the group so desires.

Examples of reflective phrases that can be used by students:

● Has role assignment during code co-writing increased productivity in solving computer programming problems?

● Was the strategy difficult to use? Because it is difficult to modify or criticize the work of others. Why?

● Was the strategy unproductive? Because the writer was very attached to his ideas, not listening to advice. Why?

● Did role assignment improve interaction among the group?

● Do we intend to continue using the role assignment strategy in future group studies?
